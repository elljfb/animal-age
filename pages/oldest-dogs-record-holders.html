The 10 Oldest Dogs Ever Recorded

Inspiring stories of canine longevity

What does it take for a dog to live past 18 or even 20 years? These records of the world's oldest dogs reveal patterns of exceptional care, fortunate genetics, and sometimes just luck. Learn about the dogs who've set longevity records and what their stories teach us about extending our pets' lifespans.

1. Bluey (1910-1939) - 29 Years, 5 Months

An Australian Cattle Dog from Queensland, Bluey holds the Guinness World Record for oldest dog ever. Owned by Les Hall, Bluey worked herding cattle and sheep for nearly 20 years before retiring. His longevity likely resulted from a combination of active lifestyle, good genetics, and outdoor living that kept him mentally and physically engaged throughout his life.

2. Chilla (1986-2009) - 27 Years, 4 Months

A Jack Russell Terrier from Tasmania, Chilla competed with Bluey's record for years before being superseded. Like Bluey, Chilla's extraordinary longevity likely came from active engagement and small breed genetics (small dogs live longer on average).

3. Coco (1990-2017) - 26 Years, 9 Months

A Toy Poodle from Orange County, California, Coco demonstrated the longevity advantage of toy breeds. Living to nearly 27 years, Coco benefited from modern veterinary care, pet food advances, and dedicated owners who provided consistent medical attention.

4. Pusuke (1985-2005) - 26 Years, 9 Months

A Shiba Inu from Okinawa, Japan, Pusuke lived in excellent health until his final years. His story highlights the importance of consistent veterinary care and the Japanese tradition of revering long-lived companion animals.

5. Bella (1992-2019) - 26 Years, 9 Months

A Chihuahua from Michigan, Bella represents the longevity advantage of tiny breeds. Despite her advanced age, Bella maintained good health and quality of life through her owner's dedicated care and consistent veterinary support.

6. August (1997-2023) - 26 Years

A recently recognized record-holder, August was a Jack Russell Terrier-Beagle mix whose extended life demonstrated the benefits of modern nutrition, preventive veterinary care, and an active, mentally stimulating lifestyle even in advanced age.

7. Toutou (1990-2016) - 26 Years

A French Fox Terrier from France, Toutou's long life reflected the benefits of European attention to pet health and well-being, including quality nutrition and regular veterinary care.

8. Max (1983-2010) - 25 Years, 7 Months

A Mixed Breed from Missouri, Max proved that exceptional longevity isn't reserved for purebreds. His owners' commitment to health maintenance, regular exercise, and proper nutrition contributed to his remarkable lifespan.

9. Spike (1991-2016) - 25 Years, 3 Months

A Chihuahua from Pennsylvania, Spike demonstrated the small-dog longevity advantage. Tiny breeds consistently dominate longevity records, suggesting that smaller body size and lower metabolic demands contribute to extended lifespans.

10. Daisy (1992-2017) - 25 Years

A Jack Russell Terrier from Australia, Daisy's extended life highlighted the breed's known longevity. Jack Russell Terriers consistently appear in longevity records, suggesting favorable genetics for long life.

What These Records Teach Us

Small Breeds Live Longer: Nearly all record holders are small breeds (Chihuahua, Jack Russell, Poodle). Small dogs age more slowly, with lower metabolic stress on organs.

Active Lifestyle Matters: Working dogs like Bluey and herding dogs featured prominently. Mental and physical engagement throughout life appears essential.

Good Genetics + Good Care: Most record holders combined favorable breeding with dedicated veterinary attention, quality nutrition, and engaged ownership.

Weight Control: Longevity record holders were generally lean. Obesity dramatically shortens lifespan and accelerates age-related diseases.

Veterinary Attention: Modern record holders benefited from regular check-ups, preventive care, and prompt treatment of health issues.

How to Help Your Dog Live Longer

  • Maintain ideal weight—this is the single biggest factor under your control
  • Provide consistent daily exercise appropriate for age and breed
  • Feed high-quality diet tailored to your dog's age and size
  • Schedule regular veterinary check-ups (twice yearly in senior years)
  • Stay mentally engaged—training, games, and puzzle toys
  • Manage stress and maintain calm, stable home environment
  • Screen for genetic health issues in breeding stock
  • Address health problems promptly rather than delaying treatment
